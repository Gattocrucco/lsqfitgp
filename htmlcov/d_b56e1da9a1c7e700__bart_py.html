96 ------- 

97 splits_from_coord

100  

101 Notes 

102 ----- 

103 This is the covariance function of the latent mean prior of BART (Bayesian 

104 Additive Regression Trees) [1]_ in the limit of an infinite number of 

105 trees, and with an upper bound :math:`D` on the depth of the trees. This 

106 prior is the distribution of the function 

107  

108 .. math:: 

109 f(\\mathbf x) = \\lim_{m\\to\\infty} 

110 \\sum_{j=1}^m g(\\mathbf x; T_j, M_j), 

111  

112 where each :math:`g(\\mathbf x; T_j, M_j)` is a decision tree evaluated at 

113 :math:`\\mathbf x`, with structure :math:`T_j` and leaf values :math:`M_j`. 

114 The trees are i.i.d., with the following distribution for :math:`T_j`: for 

115 a node at depth :math:`d`, with :math:`d = 0` for the root, the probability 

116 of not being a leaf, conditional on its existence and its ancestors only, is 

117  

118 .. math:: 

119 P_d = \\alpha (1+d)^{-\\beta}, \\quad 

120 \\alpha \\in [0, 1], \\quad \\beta \\ge 0. 

121  

122 For a non-leaf node, conditional on existence and ancestors, the splitting 

123 variable has uniform distribution amongst the variables with any splitting 

124 points not used by ancestors, and the splitting point has uniform 

125 distribution amongst the available ones. The splitting points are fixed, 

126 tipically from the data. 

127  

128 The distribution of leaves :math:`M_j` is i.i.d. Normal with variance 

129 :math:`1/m`, such that :math:`f(x)` has variance 1. In the limit 

130 :math:`m\\to\\infty`, the distribution of :math:`f(x)` becomes a Gaussian 

131 process. 

132  

133 Since the trees are independent, the covariance function can be computed 

134 for a single tree. Consider two coordinates :math:`x` and :math:`y`, with 

135 :math:`x \\le y`. Let :math:`n^-`, :math:`n^0` and :math:`n^+` be the 

136 number of splitting points respectively before :math:`x`, between 

137 :math:`x`, :math:`y` and after :math:`y`. Next, define :math:`\\mathbf 

138 n^-`, :math:`\\mathbf n^0` and :math:`\\mathbf n^+` as the vectors of such 

139 quantities for each dimension, with a total of :math:`p` dimensions, and 

140 :math:`\\mathbf n = \\mathbf n^- + \\mathbf n^0 + \\mathbf n^+`. Then the 

141 covariance function can be written recursively as 

142  

143 .. math:: 

144 \\newcommand{\\nvecs}{\\mathbf n^-, \\mathbf n^0, \\mathbf n^+} 

145 k(\\mathbf x, \\mathbf y) &= k_0(\\nvecs), \\\\ 

146 k_D(\\nvecs) &= 1 - (1 - \\gamma) P_D, 

147 \\quad \\mathbf n^0 \\ne \\mathbf 0, \\\\ 

148 k_d(\\mathbf 0, \\mathbf 0, \\mathbf 0) &= 1, \\\\ 

149 k_d(\\nvecs) &= 1 - P_d \\Bigg(1 - \\frac1{W(\\mathbf n)} 

150 \\sum_{\\substack{i=1 \\\\ n_i\\ne 0}}^p 

151 \\frac{w_i}{n_i} \\Bigg( \\\\ 

152 &\\qquad \\sum_{k=0}^{n^-_i - 1} 

153 k_{d+1}(\\mathbf n^-_{n^-_i=k}, \\mathbf n^0, \\mathbf n^+) 

154 + {} \\\\ 

155 &\\qquad \\sum_{k=0}^{n^+_i - 1} 

156 k_{d+1}(\\mathbf n^-, \\mathbf n^0, \\mathbf n^+_{n^+_i=k}) 

157 \\Bigg) 

158 \\Bigg), \\quad d < D, \\\\ 

159 W(\\mathbf n) &= \\sum_{\\substack{i=1 \\\\ n_i\\ne 0}}^p w_i. 

160  

161 The introduction of a maximum depth :math:`D` is necessary for 

162 computational feasibility. As :math:`D` increases, the result converges to 

163 the one without depth limit. For :math:`D \\le 2` (the default value), the 

164 covariance is implemented in closed form and takes :math:`O(p)` to compute. 

165 For :math:`D > 2`, the computational complexity grows exponentially as 

166 :math:`O(p(\\bar np)^{D-2})`, where :math:`\\bar n` is the average number of splitting 

167 points along a dimension. 

168  

169 In the maximum allowed depth is 1, i.e., either :math:`D = 1` or 

170 :math:`\\beta\\to\\infty`, the kernel assumes the simple form 

171  

172 .. math:: 

173 k(\\mathbf x, \\mathbf y) &= 1 - P_0 \\left( 

174 1 - Q + \\frac Q{W(\\mathbf n)} 

175 \\sum_{\\substack{i=1 \\\\ n_i\\ne 0}}^p w_i 

176 \\frac{n^0_i}{n_i} \\right), \\\\ 

177 Q &= \\begin{cases} 

178 1 - (1 - \\gamma) P_1 & \\mathbf n^0 \\ne \\mathbf 0, \\\\ 

179 1 & \\mathbf n^0 = \\mathbf 0, 

180 \\end{cases} 

181  

182 which is separable along dimensions, i.e., it has no interactions. 

183  

184 References 

185 ---------- 

186 .. [1] Hugh A. Chipman, Edward I. George, Robert E. McCulloch "BART: 

187 Bayesian additive regression trees," The Annals of Applied Statistics, 

188 Ann. Appl. Stat. 4(1), 266-298, (March 2010). 

189 """
