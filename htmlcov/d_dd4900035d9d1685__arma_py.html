70 """ 

71 Discrete autoregressive kernel. 

72  

73 You have to specify one and only one of the sets of parameters 

74 `phi+maxlag`, `gamma+maxlag`, `slnr+lnc`. 

75 

76 Parameters 

77 ---------- 

78 phi : (p,) real 

79 The autoregressive coefficients at lag 1...p. 

80 gamma : (p + 1,) real 

81 The autocovariance at lag 0...p. 

82 maxlag : int 

83 The maximum lag that the kernel will be evaluated on. If the actual 

84 inputs produce higher lags, the missing values are filled with ``nan``. 

85 slnr : (nr,) real 

86 The real roots of the characteristic polynomial, expressed in the 

87 following way: ``sign(slnr)`` is the sign of the root, and 

88 ``abs(snlr)`` is the natural logarithm of the absolute value. 

89 lnc : (nc,) complex 

90 The natural logarithm of the complex roots of the characteristic 

91 polynomial (:math:`\\log z = \\log|z| + i\\arg z`), where each root 

92 also stands for its paired conjugate. 

93  

94 In `slnr` and `lnc`, the multiplicity of a root is expressed by 

95 repeating the root in the array (not necessarily next to each other). 

96 Only exact repetition counts; very close yet distinct roots are treated 

97 as separate and lead to numerical instability, in particular complex 

98 roots very close to the real line. An exactly real complex root behaves 

99 like a pair of identical real roots. Two complex roots also count as 

100 equal if conjugate, and the argument is standardized to :math:`[0, 

101 2\\pi)`. 

102 norm : bool 

103 If True, normalize the autocovariance to be 1 at lag 0. If False 

104 (default), normalize such that the variance of the generating noise is 

105 1, or use the user-provided normalization if `gamma` is specified. 

106  

107 Notes 

108 ----- 

109 This is the covariance function of a stationary autoregressive process, 

110 which is defined recursively as 

111  

112 .. math:: 

113 y_i = \\sum_{k=1}^p \\phi_k y_{i-k} + \\epsilon_i, 

114  

115 where :math:`\\epsilon_i` is white noise, i.e., 

116 :math:`\\operatorname{Cov}[\\epsilon_i, \\epsilon_j] = \\delta_{ij}`. The 

117 length :math:`p` of the vector of coefficients :math:`\\boldsymbol\\phi` 

118 is the `order` of the process. 

119  

120 The covariance function can be expressed in two ways. First as the same 

121 recursion defining the process: 

122  

123 .. math:: 

124 \\gamma_m = \\sum_{k=1}^p \\phi_k \\gamma_{m-k} + \\delta_{m0}, 

125  

126 where :math:`\\gamma_m \\equiv \\operatorname{Cov}[y_i, y_{i+m}]`. This is 

127 called `Yule-Walker equation`. Second, as a linear combination of mixed 

128 power-exponentials: 

129  

130 .. math:: 

131 \\gamma_m = \\sum_{j=1}^n 

132 \\sum_{l=1}^{\\mu_j} 

133 a_{jl} |m|^{l-1} x_j^{-|m|}, 

134  

135 where :math:`x_j` and :math:`\\mu_j` are the (complex) roots and 

136 corresponding multiplicities of the `characteristic polynomial` 

137  

138 .. math:: 

139 P(x) = 1 - \\sum_{k=1}^p \\phi_k x^k, 

140  

141 and the :math:`a_{jl}` are uniquely determined complex coefficients. The 

142 :math:`\\boldsymbol\\phi` vector is valid iff :math:`|x_j|>1, \\forall j`. 

143  

144 There are three alternative parametrization for this kernel. 

145  

146 If you specify `phi`, the first terms of the covariance are computed 

147 solving the Yule-Walker equation, and then evolved up to `maxlag`. It 

148 is necessary to specify `maxlag` instead of letting the code figure it out 

149 from the actual inputs for technical reasons. 

150  

151 Likewise, if you specify `gamma`, the coefficients are obtained with 

152 Yule-Walker and then used to evolve the covariance. The only difference is 

153 that the normalization can be different: starting from `phi`, the variance 

154 of the generating noise :math:`\\epsilon` is fixed to 1, while giving 

155 `gamma` directly implies an arbitrary value. 

156  

157 Instead, if you specify the roots with `slnr` and `lnc`, the coefficients 

158 are obtained from the polynomial defined in terms of the roots, and then 

159 the amplitudes :math:`a_{jl}` are computed by solving a linear system with 

160 the covariance (from YW) as RHS. Finally, the full covariance function is 

161 evaluated with the analytical expression. 

162  

163 The reasons for using the logarithm are that 1) in practice the roots are 

164 tipically close to 1, so the logarithm is numerically more accurate, and 2) 

165 the logarithm is readily interpretable as the inverse of the correlation 

166 length. 

167  

168 """
